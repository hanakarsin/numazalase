At least 24 county assemblies expected to pass BBI Bill this month as Siaya leads the way

April 2024 · 5 minute read

- At least 10 county assemblies in Mount Kenya region pledged to pass the BBI Bill in the coming days

- Unanimous passing of the bill in Siaya set the stage for approval of the document in other Nyanza counties

- Nairobi MCAs also vowed to pass the BBI Bill without delay because of the many benefits it has for devolution

- BBI secretariat is confident the requisite 24 counties will have endorsed the bill by the end of the month

Proponents of the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) are confident that at least 24 county assemblies will approve the Constitution of Kenya (Amendment Bill) by the end of this month as the journey to a referendum enters the homestretch.

The assuredness was given credence on Wednesday, February 3, after the Siaya county assembly passed the BBI Bill after less than two hours of debate, setting the pace for the rest of the assemblies to ratify the document in the coming days.

The unanimous endorsement of the bill in Siaya, which is Raila Odinga’s home county, was a shot in the arm for the constitutional reforms process as it signalled the overwhelming support for BBI in the ODM leader’s political strongholds.

The document is expected to easily sail through in all the six Nyanza counties where the bill has been placed top on the list of agenda items.

Kisumu county is expected to approve the bill when it comes up for debate next week. It was tabled before the county assembly this week.

Over the weekend, at least 10 county assemblies in Mt Kenya region promised they will pass the BBI Bill, boosting the chances of the document being approved by at least 24 counties assemblies as required by law for the process to move to the next stage.

The counties are Meru, Tharaka Nithi, Embu, Kirinyaga, Nyeri, Murang'a, Nyandarua, Kiambu, Laikipia and Nakuru.

Over 550 MCAs from the region made the declaration during a meeting convened by President Uhuru Kenyatta at Sagana State Lodge where he personally drummed up support for the handshake document project and explained the many social, political and economic issues bedevilling the country that the initiative will address.

Benefits of BBI at grassroots

Among the projected benefits of the BBI at the grassroots level will be the increase of funds allocated to counties as well as the creation of additional constituencies, which will take resources closer to Wanjiku.

These benefits have seen Nairobi county sssembly MCAs vow to pass the BBI Bill with or without sweeteners such as car grants.

“We have resolved to pass BBI with or without car grants, for we believe it has many benefits for devolution. Likewise, I am asking my counterparts countrywide to pass the Bill for the benefit of the future generation,” the ward reps said on Sunday, January 30, led by Deputy Majority Whip Waithera Chege.

BBI secretariat co-chairman Dennis Waweru said the historic move by Siaya county is a “sign of things to come” and more counties will follow suit.

“We expect to have attained a majority by the end of the month,” he told Daily Nation.

Once at least 24 county assemblies pass the Bill, it will be officially introduced in the Senate and the National Assembly, a stage that is widely considered a formality.

The proposed amendment will be forwarded to the president for assent if a majority of the members of each House approve it.

BBI Referendum

But the same will be submitted to the people in a referendum if either House of Parliament fails to pass the Bill, or if the Bill relates to a matter specified in Article 255 (1) of the Constitution.

Under Article 255(1), some areas of the Constitution must be subjected to a plebiscite if they are to be changed.

They include areas touching on the supremacy of the Constitution, the territory of Kenya, the sovereignty of the people, the Bill of Rights, the term of office of the President and the functions of Parliament as well as that on the objects, principles and structure of devolved government.

Others are areas touching on the national values and principles of governance referred to in Article 10 (2) (a) to (d), the independence of the Judiciary and the commissions and independent offices to which Chapter Fifteen applies, as well as the provisions of the Chapter on amending the Constitution.

Some provisions of the BBI touch on these areas, meaning a referendum is inevitable. A proposed amendment is passed in a referendum when at least 20% of the registered voters in each of at least 24 counties vote in the referendum and the amendment garners the support of a simple majority of the voters.
