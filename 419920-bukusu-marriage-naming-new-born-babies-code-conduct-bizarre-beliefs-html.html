Bukusu is a subtribe of the Luhya community, one of the largest tribes in East and Central Africa. The community is mostly found in Bungoma and Trans Nzoia counties.

The community boasts of unique indigenous cultural practices that will leave you fascinated.

With this in mind, you must understand that Bukusu's are exciting people with a rich cultural heritage and diversified practices from circumcision to marriage and death.

First, one needs to understand this culture because some are breathtaking; you need the courage to perform some of this culture though one might find them interesting at the end.

It should not go without saying that Bukusu elders are considered the pillar guiding and presiding over cultural practices; performing culture is broad, rituals and cleansing are part of this culture.

With the ongoing efforts to curb tribalism in the country through intermarriage, Kenyans need to understand a few things before committing to either of the 42 tribes.

1. Burial rights

Bukusu's have a lot of respect for the dead; notably, they name siblings born after the death of their beloved once.

However, the Bukusu community do not name kids after barren women or men; once a barren person dies, they chose to have the dead rest without disturbance.

A special ritual is done to ensure the dead does not spell a barren curse to the generation.

Additionally, if husbands lost their wives during burial ceremonies, they cannot mingle with in-laws who are sisters to the deceased. Bukusus believes that in-laws, once they step on the toes of a grieving husband, might not remarry again.

Interestingly, if a woman dies when the husband is yet to pay dowry, he will be forced to pay dowry before being allowed by the deceased's family to bury the spouse.

2. Shaking hands with in-laws 

Bukusus don't shake hands to greet in-laws; this is an abomination according to Luhya traditional belief.

However, shaking hands with mother-in-law and daughters-in-law were regarded as a sign of respect in the past.

This explains why Bukusus have the best relationship between two sides of in-laws.

However, according to the culture, once the two shake hands knowing or unknowingly, a special ritual must be performed for cleansing.

3. Age sets 

Referring to people who were circumcised in the same year is commonly known as 'Bakule' among the Kalenjin community.

But in Bukusu, there are eight age sets known as 'Bibingilo'.

The age set group earn themselves respect to an extent; they can discipline wives and kids of their members. However, no one is to overrule what another member has said. 

On one occasion, a lady ran away from a violent husband but was stopped and returned by age set member to the violent husband.

This group that was circumcised together have a lot of respect for one another.

4. Specific food not allowed at in-laws until you pay dowry

TUKO.co.ke understands that some food are prepared for specific functions in Bukusuland.

Eliud Khaoya from Nakayonjo village in Mukuyuni ward Kabuchai constituency told TUKO.co.ke that eating chicken at your in-laws, especially when you are yet to pay dowry, is taboo.

He further added that one is not supposed to eat chicken at her boyfriend's home when your fiancee visits you before one gets married.

"Chicken is very significant among Bukusu and Luhya community at large, for instance; Bukusu believe that when your husband who is yet to pay dowry visits your parents and eat chicken, he might not pay dowry at all. Consequently, if you visit your spouse and be served with chicken, chances of two of you breaking up is high," said Khaoya

5. Polygamous (recognizing the first wife)

The community recognise first wives when prioritising issues within the family. A good example is when a husband dies.

The person who is allowed to bury him is the first wife. Elijah Mwangale, a traditional overseer from Ndalu in the Tongaren constituency, explains many issues concerning first wife importance.

"First wives are a pillar to any family, and therefore the community recognizes them very well. In the event where a husband dies, it's the first wife to bury him. Again where inheritance is done, if the husband had five wives, the subdivision is done on pro-rata basis, the first wive will always get the biggest share as it trickles downwards,'' said Mwangale.

6. Naming of newborn babies 

This is done by either aunties and grandparents of the partanal family. This is important in the Luhya community at large.

Giving names pleases ancestors; when a newborn baby cries during the day, it signifies that the kid should be given the name from her mother's side; when the kid cries at night, the ancestors from the paternal side will be demanding to be named.

7. First child shave

Shaving newborn babies is a sign of acceptance; grandmothers and aunties only do this.

This shows that your kid has been accepted in the family. In the past, children born out of wedlock could die after being shaved by people who are not their siblings.

Bukusus believed that a baby would die if not shaved by someone, not of the same blood.

8. Giving birth to firstborn twins

This is considered a bad omen; in the larger Luhya community, older adults believe that giving birth to twins, especially firstborn, is a sign of bad luck in marriage.

According to tradition, one twin will be killed, though this no longer happens with the coming of churches and new religions. You can easily track down names given to twins in the community.

However, most of them in their 50s don't have their twin siblings, and their history is not clear.

9. Bringing the dead spirit home

The community does believe in bringing the spirit of members of the family who died in accidents and foreign lands home.

For instance, if one succumbs to an accident while in Mombasa, elders and family members will perform a special ritual to bring their spirit home.

This is usually done after the dead has ''brought dreams'' to close family members and relatives.

10. Feeding the dead ones

This is one of the fascinating parts of Bukusu culture.

According to tradition, a family is not supposed to finish all food they take for dinner.

This is because it is believed the dead spirits usually visit their loved ones once, and they should find something to eat.
